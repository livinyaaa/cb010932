CEYLON TEA INDUSTRY

Introduced in 1867, Ceylon Tea has grown to be the top agriculture export in Sri Lanka and provides direct and indirect employment to nearly 1 million people while around 4% of the country’s land area is covered in tea plantations amounting to nearly 203000 hectares. The country produces tea throughout the year and the total tea production is about 340 million kilograms per annum.

TEA PLANTATIONS

Prominent Tea Regions In Ceylon

CEYLON'S HIGH-GROWN TEA PLANTATIONS

The elevations of high-grown Ceylon teas range from 400 to 6000 feet above sea level. Due to the constant cool and windy climate, these teas, which account for the majority of Ceylon's tea production, have a stronger aroma compared to others and are lighter in flavor and color. Nuwara Eliya, Uda Pussellawa and Dimbulla are the main high-grown tea regions of the country.

CEYLON'S MID-GROWN TEA PLANTATIONS

Sri Lankan mid-grown teas are primarily found in and around the Kandy district, at elevations of around 2000 feet. They have a rich flavor, a medium body, and a brisk, light taste. Mid-grown teas are distinguished by their bright burgundy color. The peak season for growth of these teas is in the spring, but unlike high and low-growngrown teas, these bushes grow all year due to the low monsoon impact.  Mid-grown teas are commonly found in the Central and Uva provinces of Sri Lanka.

CEYLON'S LOW-GROWN TEA PLANTATIONS

Low-grown teas account for the majority of Ceylon tea production and are also the fastest growing. The teas are grown at a height of about 300 feet above sea level. They have a distinct dark color and strong flavor due to the region's extreme heat and humidity levels. You can find tea plantations that belong to the low-grown tea category in the Sabaragamuwa and Southern provinces of Sri Lanka.

CEYLON TEA MUSEUM

A Heritage Of Excellence

EXPERIENCE OVER 200 YEARS OF CEYLON TEA

Situated around four kilometres to the south of Kandy, the Ceylon Tea Museum occupies a building that was once the Hatane Tea Factory, constructed in 1925. The tea factory had been abandoned for over a decade before the Chairman of the Sri Lanka Tea Board, Clifford Ratwatte, incorporated the Ceylon Tea Museum into it in 1998. The Tea Board and the Planters’ Association of Sri Lanka ensured that the building was refurbished for its new designation as a museum, and it was formally opened to the public on the 1st of December in 2001.
